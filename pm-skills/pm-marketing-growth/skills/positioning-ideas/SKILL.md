# Positioning Ideas

Use when: the user wants to define how their product should be positioned in the market, differentiate from competitors, or craft a positioning strategy for a new category or segment.

Trigger: "positioning", "how should we position", "market positioning", "positioning strategy", "category creation", "rebranding", "differentiation strategy"

## Framework (April Dunford — Obviously Awesome)

Positioning answers the question: "Why should a specific customer care about your product, compared to their alternatives?"

It's not a tagline. It's a strategic choice about:
- Who you compete with in the customer's mind
- What makes you different from those alternatives
- Why those differences matter to specific customers

### The Five Components of Positioning

#### 1. Competitive Alternatives
What would the customer do if your product didn't exist?
- Direct competitors (obvious)
- Indirect substitutes (spreadsheet, manual process, do nothing)
- Status quo (the real competitor in many markets)

#### 2. Unique Attributes
What do you have that alternatives genuinely don't?
- Features, capabilities, or combinations
- Technology differentiators
- Team expertise or data
- Network effects or integrations

#### 3. Value for the Customer
For each unique attribute, what does it mean for the customer?
- "Attribute X means that customers can [outcome Y]"
- Translate features into customer-felt benefits

#### 4. Target Customer Segment
Which customers value your unique attributes most?
- Who feels the most pain that your attributes solve?
- Who has the most to gain from your specific differentiation?

#### 5. Market Frame of Reference
What category do you compete in — or do you create a new one?
- Existing category (compete within it)
- Subcategory (niche down with a modifier)
- New category (educate the market)

### Four Positioning Approaches

**Approach 1: Head-to-Head**
"We do what [category leader] does, but better in [specific dimension]."
Use when: You have a clear superiority on a dimension that matters.

**Approach 2: Niche Domination**
"We're built specifically for [narrow segment] — [category leaders] are built for everyone."
Use when: The market leader serves multiple segments and leaves gaps.

**Approach 3: New Category**
"Forget [existing category] — that's [old way]. [Product] is [new category] — [new way of solving problem]."
Use when: Your product enables something truly new that existing categories don't capture.

**Approach 4: Value-Based Repositioning**
Shift the competition from features to outcomes.
"Don't choose based on features — choose based on results. We guarantee [outcome]."
Use when: You have evidence of superior outcomes vs. feature-parity competitors.

## Output Format

1. **Competitive alternatives**: What customers compare you to (list with notes)
2. **Unique attributes**: Your genuine differentiators (3–5)
3. **Value translation**: Each attribute → customer benefit (table)
4. **Target segment**: Who values your differentiation most
5. **Positioning approaches**: Generate one option per approach
6. **Recommended positioning**: Best approach with rationale
7. **Positioning statement** (Geoffrey Moore template):
   ```
   For [target segment] who [have this need],
   [Product] is a [category] that [unique benefit].
   Unlike [primary alternative], [Product] [key differentiator].
   ```
8. **Messages to avoid**: Positions that would be ineffective or misleading
